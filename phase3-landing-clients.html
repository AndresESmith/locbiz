Step 1 of 5 — Targeting

Who to Target First

Not every business is worth approaching. These six types have the highest need, lowest bar to close, and will pay reasonable prices without needing an extensive pitch.

Barbershops & Salons
⚡ High need, fast decisions

Often run by a single owner who makes decisions instantly. Booking systems, galleries, and service menus = clear value. Many still have no site or a terrible Facebook page as their "site."

avg: $600–900
Restaurants & Cafés
⚡ Menu + hours = immediate value

Every restaurant gets asked "do you have a menu online?" If the answer is no, they're losing customers daily. Owners understand the ROI immediately when you frame it this way.

avg: $700–1,000
Contractors & Trades
⭐ High value, less competition

Plumbers, electricians, painters, landscapers. These guys get jobs worth $500–5,000 — a $1,000 website that gets them even one extra job pays for itself. They're motivated by ROI.

avg: $800–1,200
Auto Shops & Detailers
✓ Easy to pitch before/after

Before/after car photos are incredibly shareable. Easy to show a gallery. Owners tend to be practical and respond well to "here's what it would look like" mockups.

avg: $500–800
Personal Trainers & Gyms
⚡ Booking + testimonials = perfect

Trainers need bookings, testimonials, and a professional presence. Many market themselves on Instagram but have no website to link. Easy upsell on scheduling integrations.

avg: $500–900
Cleaning & Home Services
✓ Easy referral loop

Home cleaners, dog walkers, lawn care. These businesses run on local referrals. A professional site with reviews and a quote form can double their inbound leads. They often have steady recurring revenue and will pay consistently for a retainer.

avg: $450–750
✓ How to find them without walking in

Google Maps is your lead list. Search "[business type] [your city]" — filter for businesses with no website listed or ones linked to a basic Facebook page. Those are your leads. Write down 20 per session. This takes 30 minutes and gives you a week's worth of outreach targets.

Step 2 of 5 — Channels

Your 4 Outreach Channels

Use all four. Each reaches a different kind of client. Rank them by effort and use accordingly.

Channel 01
Walk-In (In Person)

The highest-converting channel by far. Walk into local businesses during off-peak hours (weekday afternoons). Show your portfolio on your phone. Ask to speak with the owner. Your age makes you memorable — owners rarely turn away a motivated teenager with a professional portfolio.

Highest ROI 5 walk-ins/week
Channel 02
Warm Network (People You Know)

Tell every adult you know: parents, relatives, neighbors, coaches, teachers. The message: "I'm building websites for local businesses — do you know anyone who needs one?" You need one introduction, not a sale. Warm referrals close 5x faster than cold outreach.

Easiest closes Tell 20 people total
Channel 03
Cold Email

Find business emails on Google Maps or their existing (bad) website. Send a short, 4-sentence email with one specific observation about their business and a mockup screenshot. Volume is key — expect 5–10% reply rate. Never send a wall of text. The script below is your template.

Medium effort 10 emails/week
Channel 04
Social Media / Community Posts

Post in local Facebook groups, Nextdoor, and community Subreddits. Frame it honestly: "I'm a 15-year-old building websites for local businesses. First client this month gets 20% off." These posts regularly go mini-viral locally because the "15 years old" angle is compelling. Post once a week, different groups.

Passive leads 1 post/week
Step 3 of 5 — Scripts

Word-for-Word Scripts

Memorize these. Adapt to your voice. Practice out loud before using them.

WALK-IN SCRIPT — Say this at the door
"Hi, is the owner around?" [when owner appears] "Hi, my name's [Your Name] — I'm a local student and I design websites for small businesses. I was nearby and noticed you don't have a website yet, or it looks like it might be due for an update. I actually put together a quick concept of what yours could look like — would you have 2 minutes to take a look? I've already built a few sites for businesses in the area and I'd love to help you get found online." [show your phone portfolio] "I'm 15, so my rates are very reasonable compared to agencies — and I'm available locally. Would you want to chat more about what you're looking for?"
💡 If they say "I'm busy" → "Of course — could I leave my card and you take a look when you have 2 minutes?" Then hand your phone number on a notecard.
COLD EMAIL SCRIPT — 4 sentences max
Subject: Quick website idea for [Business Name] Hi [Owner Name or "there"], I noticed [Business Name] doesn't have a website yet — I Googled you and couldn't find one, which means you're probably missing customers who search online. I'm a local web designer (I'm 15, so my prices are much lower than an agency) and I built a quick concept of what your site could look like — I'd love to show you. I've attached a screenshot. Would you be open to a quick 10-minute call this week? [Your Name] | [yourname.com] | [phone/email]
💡 Attach a screenshot of your most relevant portfolio piece (same industry). Subject line with their business name dramatically improves open rates.
WARM REFERRAL ASK — Text or say in person
Text to a family friend or adult you know: "Hey [Name] — random question: do you know anyone who owns a small business that doesn't have a website, or has a really outdated one? I've started building websites for local businesses and I'm looking for my next client. Even a quick intro would help a ton." --- Or at a family event / conversation: "I've started a little side business making websites for local businesses. Honestly the hardest part is just finding people who need them — if you ever hear of anyone, I'd love an intro."
💡 Don't ask them to "buy" anything — just an introduction. This removes all pressure and makes people far more likely to help.
FACEBOOK / NEXTDOOR POST
📣 Local business owners — I build websites Hi [Town Name]! I'm a 15-year-old high school student who builds professional websites for local businesses. I've been doing this for a few months and I'd love to help more businesses in our community get found online. My work: [yourname.com] I offer: → Starter sites from $400 → Full 5-page sites from $800 → Monthly maintenance for $150/mo First new client this month gets 20% off. Feel free to DM me or comment below — happy to give a free quote!
💡 Post this in 3–4 local Facebook groups or Nextdoor neighborhoods. Rotate groups weekly so you're not spamming the same feed.
Step 4 of 5 — Objections

Handle Every Objection

These are the exact responses you'll hear. Know them before you walk in the door.

❓

"You're only 15 — can you actually handle this?"

✓

"That's fair — my work speaks for itself." [Pull up your phone portfolio] "I've already built [X] sites — these are all live. I think you'll find the quality is what matters, not my age. Most of my clients are actually happy I'm younger because I'm more up to date on what works online right now."

❓

"We already have a website."

✓

"That's great — do you mind if I take a quick look?" [Look it up on your phone] If it's old/basic: "I think there's a real opportunity to modernize this — mobile usage is huge and this one might not be converting as well as it could. Would you be open to a free audit of what could be improved?"

❓

"I can't afford it right now."

✓

"Totally understand. What if we started small — a simple 2-page site that gets you online and looking professional? I can do that for $[lower price]. And if you ever want to expand it, we can do that down the road. The important thing is getting you found online."

❓

"I need to think about it / ask my partner."

✓

"Of course — take your time. Would it help if I sent you a written quote with exactly what's included? That way you have something concrete to look at together. What's the best email to send it to?" [Get their email. Follow up in 3 days with a short note + quote.]

❓

"My nephew/friend can do it for free."

✓

"That's great if it works out! The difference is I do this professionally and on a timeline — you get a proper contract, a set delivery date, and ongoing support. Free often means 'whenever they get around to it' and no accountability. But if they come through, that's totally fine — I just wanted to make sure you knew your options."

Step 5 of 5 — Consistency

Your Weekly Outreach Routine

This takes 5–7 hours per week on top of any active project work. The key is doing it every week — not in bursts.

Day Action Detail
Monday Research 15 leads Search Google Maps for [business type] in your area. Note name, address, email, and whether they have a site. Build this list weekly in a Notion or Google Sheet.
Tuesday Send 10 cold emails Use the script. Personalize the first line for each business (mention their specific gap). Attach a relevant portfolio screenshot. Send from a professional email — yourname@yourname.com via Google Workspace or just Gmail.
Wednesday 3–4 walk-ins Go after school or on a free period. Hit businesses in the same neighborhood to save time. Wear something presentable. Bring a small card with your name and website URL.
Thursday Follow up + post Follow up on any emails from Tuesday with no reply ("Just wanted to make sure this didn't get lost!"). Post your weekly social media blurb in 2–3 local Facebook groups or Nextdoor.
Friday Warm network check-in Text or ask 2–3 people in your network: "Anyone you know needing a website?" Also follow up on any outstanding quotes or conversations from the week.
Weekend Review + project work Count your contacts this week (aim for 15+ total touchpoints). Work on any active client projects. Update your portfolio with anything new. Rest — you're doing great.
🔥 The most important mindset

You will hear "no" or get no response from 9 out of 10 people. That's completely normal and has nothing to do with your quality. A plumber reaches 100 houses before getting 10 jobs. Treat outreach like a math equation: more inputs = more outputs. The goal each week is contacts, not closes.

📊 Track everything in a simple spreadsheet

Keep one Google Sheet with columns: Business Name | Contact Method | Date | Follow-up Date | Status (Contacted / Replied / Meeting / Closed / Dead). Review it every Friday. A managed pipeline is the difference between freelancers who get stuck and ones who grow consistently.
