Step 1 of 5

Choose Your Building Tool

You don't need to code from scratch. Pick one platform and master it completely — don't split your attention.

Recommended for beginners
Webflow

Visual drag-and-drop builder with real HTML/CSS output. Steep learning curve for the first 3 days, then clicks fast. Has a free plan. Clients get a professional CMS. The industry standard for freelancers.

✓ Best for long-term — most hireable skill too
Fastest results
Framer

More modern, better animations out of the box, slightly easier to learn. Slightly less flexible for complex layouts. Free plan available. Growing fast in popularity — great portfolio aesthetic.

✓ Pick this if you want results in 3 days, not 7
Easiest, but limited
Squarespace / Wix

Fastest to learn, hardest to customize. Sites look templated. Harder to justify a premium price. Fine to start, but upgrade to Webflow or Framer as soon as possible.

⚠ Only if you want first client in days, not weeks
For the ambitious
HTML + CSS + JS

Takes 1–3 months to get good enough to show clients. Not recommended for Phase 1. Learn it alongside your tool if interested, but don't delay client work to "learn to code first."

⚠ Do this after your first $1,000 — not before
📺 Where to learn (free)

For Webflow: search "Webflow University" on YouTube — official channel, totally free, covers everything. For Framer: "Framer Academy" YouTube. Commit to 2–3 hours of tutorials on Day 1 before touching anything else.

Step 2 of 5

Learn Figma for Client Mockups

Figma is how you show clients what their site will look like before you build it. This alone makes you look 10x more professional.

1

Create a free Figma account

Go to figma.com — the free plan is all you need. Download the desktop app for better performance. Don't pay for anything yet.

15 minutes
2

Watch "Figma for Beginners" on YouTube

Search "Figma for beginners web design 2024" — pick any video over 45 minutes. You need to learn: frames, auto layout, components, and how to share a prototype link. These 4 things are all you need.

2–3 hours
3

Clone a free Figma website template

Search "free Figma website template" in the Figma Community. Clone one, take it apart, and study how it's structured. This is faster than building from scratch as a beginner.

1 hour
4

Build one mockup from scratch

Design a homepage for a fake local business (e.g., "Mike's Barbershop"). Include: hero section, services, about, contact. This becomes portfolio piece #1 even before you build the real site.

3–4 hours
💡 Why this matters

When you meet a client, you can say: "I'll show you exactly what your site will look like before I build a single page." This closes deals. Clients hate surprises — Figma mockups eliminate them. It also protects you from doing full rebuilds because the client "didn't picture it that way."

Step 3 of 5

Build 3 Portfolio Sites

You need proof of your work before you have real clients. Build fictional but realistic sites for three different local business types.

Site #1
Suggested: Barbershop / Hair Salon
  • Hero with big photo + "Book Now" button
  • Services + pricing section
  • Gallery of 6 haircut photos (from Unsplash)
  • Hours + location + Google Map embed
  • Contact form
  • Mobile responsive — test on your phone
Site #2
Suggested: Restaurant / Café
  • Full-bleed food photography hero
  • Menu section (PDF link or inline)
  • About the owner / story section
  • Reservation / contact form
  • Instagram feed embed (or fake grid)
  • Hours + location + parking info
Site #3
Suggested: Contractor / Plumber / Cleaner
  • Trust-first hero: "Licensed & Insured"
  • Services list with icons
  • Before/after project photos
  • 3 fake testimonials (star rating style)
  • Call-to-action: "Get a free quote"
  • Contact form + phone number
⚠ Important: Label these as "Concept Designs"

In your portfolio, write "Concept design — fictional business" in small text below each site. This is honest and legal. Clients won't care — they're looking at the quality of the work, not whether the business is real. Never imply fake clients are real clients.

🖼 Free photo sources (critical for quality)

Your sites look professional when photos look professional. Use these free sources — never use Google Images (copyright issues):

  • Unsplash.com — best quality, any category
  • Pexels.com — large library, easy search
  • Pixabay.com — more stock-style but free
  • Search "[business type] [food/interior/people]" — be specific
Step 4 of 5 · Optional but highly recommended

Land One Real (Free or Cheap) Client

One real project outweighs three fictional ones. It's also how you learn what clients actually want.

1

List every adult you know

Parents, relatives, neighbors, parents' friends, coaches, teachers with side businesses. Write them all down. You need 10 names minimum.

Day 1
2

Ask each one a simple question

"Do you know anyone who owns a small business without a website, or with a really outdated one?" Don't pitch yourself yet — just gather names. You're looking for an introduction, not a sale.

Days 1–3
3

Offer to do it free or for $100–200

This is a portfolio project, not a money-making project. Frame it that way: "I'm building my portfolio and I'd love to do your website for free / for a small fee. All I ask is a testimonial when it's done." Most small business owners will say yes instantly.

Days 3–5
4

Build it, get their feedback, publish it

Show them the Figma mockup first. Get one round of feedback. Build it. Show them the live site. Get a written testimonial and a screenshot permission. This is now your strongest portfolio piece — a real business, real URL.

Week 2
Step 5 of 5

Build Your Own Portfolio Website

Your site is your proof of concept. If your own website looks bad, clients won't trust you to build theirs.

1

Choose a domain name

Use your real name: firstname-lastname.com or firstnamelastname.com. If your name is hard to spell, use a business name: "BrightSiteStudios.com" or similar. Buy through Namecheap (~$10/yr) or Google Domains.

Cost: ~$10
2

Your site needs exactly these 5 things

1. Your name + what you do ("I build websites for local businesses")
2. 3 portfolio pieces with live links or screenshots
3. A short "About" — including that you're 15 (this is your edge, own it)
4. A contact form or "Book a call" button (use Calendly free)
5. 1–2 testimonials once you have them

3–5 hours to build
3

Keep it dead simple

One page is fine. Resist the urge to build an elaborate site for yourself — clients don't hire you because your site has parallax scrolling. They hire you because your portfolio work is good and you seem professional and responsive.

Less is more
4

Test it on mobile before showing anyone

Over 60% of people will view your site on a phone. If it looks broken on mobile, you've lost the client before you even spoke to them. Test on both iOS and Android if possible.

30 min test + fix session

Your Phase 1 Completion Checklist

  • I chose one building tool (Webflow, Framer, or Squarespace) and completed the beginner tutorial series
  • I can use Figma to design a basic 5-section homepage mockup
  • I have 3 portfolio sites built (fictional is fine) with real photography from Unsplash/Pexels
  • Each portfolio site is mobile-responsive and tested on a phone
  • I have (or am in progress on) 1 real client site, even if free
  • My personal portfolio site is live at a real domain (yourname.com)
  • My portfolio site has a working contact form or Calendly booking link
  • I have at least 1 written testimonial (if I did the free client project)
